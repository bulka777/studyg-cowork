01 — WHY IT MATTERS

When retrieval fails, the problem is usually the chunks.

Retrieval-augmented generation only works if the retriever surfaces the right context. Before any document is embedded and stored, it has to be split into chunks, and those cut points define the smallest unit your system can ever retrieve. If a key fact is split across two chunks, or buried in a chunk dominated by unrelated text, no embedding model or reranker can fully recover it.

Weaviate's September 2025 chunking guide reports that the wrong chunking strategy can create a gap of up to 9% in recall between the best and worst approaches on the same corpus and retriever — a large swing that costs nothing but a configuration change.
— Weaviate Engineering Team, Chunking Strategies for RAG, Sept 2025

There is a useful heuristic: Pinecone's June 2025 guide calls it the human readability rule — if a chunk makes sense to a human without its surrounding context, it will usually make sense to the language model too. That single test catches most of the worst chunking failures: fragments that start mid-sentence, tables sliced in half, or list items severed from the heading that gives them meaning.

04 — THE OVERLAP MYTH

The default nobody re-tests.

Almost every chunking guide recommends a 10–20% overlap between adjacent chunks as a universal default. Pinecone, for instance, suggests 512 tokens with a 50–100 token overlap. The reasoning is sound on its face — so it rarely gets re-tested.

A January 2026 systematic analysis on arXiv challenged that assumption directly. Using SPLADE retrieval with an 8B Mistral model on the Natural Questions dataset, authors Bennani & Moslonka varied token, sentence, and semantic chunking across chunk sizes, overlap settings, and context lengths. Their finding on overlap: it provided no measurable benefit in their tested setup and only increased indexing cost.
— Bennani & Moslonka, arXiv:2601.14123, January 2026

The honest reading is not that overlap is always harmful — it is that the evidence questions the universal overlap rule. Overlap may still earn its keep in boundary-sensitive domains like legal clauses. The right posture: treat overlap as a tunable you test, not a setting you copy from a tutorial.

05 — CHUNK SIZE

The sweet spot and the context cliff.

There is now converging evidence on a defensible range. An October 2023 LlamaIndex study on Uber's 10-K found 1024 tokens produced peak faithfulness with only modest response-time cost. A February 2026 vendor benchmark ranked recursive 512-token splitting first at 69% accuracy across seven strategies.

The same January 2026 arXiv analysis (Bennani & Moslonka) added a finding most guides lack: a context cliff at roughly 2,500 tokens, beyond which response quality degraded. BERTScore declined ~4–5% relatively at 10k tokens for sentence chunking. It also found sentence-based chunking matched semantic chunking up to ~5,000 tokens at a fraction of the cost.
— Bennani & Moslonka, arXiv:2601.14123, January 2026
The 2,500-token cliff comes from a single preprint using one retriever (SPLADE) and one generator (Mistral-8B) on Natural Questions. Treat it as a directional warning, not a universal constant.
— Digital Applied Team, May 2026

A November 2025 peer-reviewed MDPI Bioengineering study found adaptive topic-boundary chunking reached 87% accuracy versus just 13% for a fixed-size baseline in clinical decision support — showing the direction generalises: when your documents have strong inherent structure, cut along that structure.

09 — THE DECISION PATH

What to actually do.

Step 1 — Default: recursive 512-token splits. Use from_tiktoken_encoder() for token-accurate cuts. Skip overlap as a default.

Step 2 — Match size to query type. Factoid lookups: 64–256 tokens. Analytical/narrative: 512–1024+.

Step 3 — Graduate to semantic/hierarchical when metrics justify it. ~14× slower; earn the complexity via RAGAS scores.

Step 4 — Add Contextual Retrieval or late chunking for high-value corpora. For corpora under ~200k tokens, consider skipping RAG entirely and injecting the full document.
— Digital Applied Team, May 2026

Background & History

Why does chunking exist at all? The story starts with a fundamental constraint.

The core problem

Why you can't just feed the whole document to the model

Language models have a context window — a hard limit on how many tokens they can process in one call. In 2022, GPT-3 had a 4,096-token window. That's roughly 10 pages of text. A corporate knowledge base might have thousands of documents. You can't fit them all.

The retrieval-before-generation idea: Instead of shoving everything into the prompt, what if you retrieved just the relevant pieces first? This is the insight behind RAG. But now a new question appears: what is "a piece"? You need to decide the unit of retrieval before you can retrieve anything. That unit is the chunk.

Chunking is therefore not an optimisation detail — it is the foundational decision in any retrieval system. The chunk is the atom. Everything else (embedding, indexing, retrieval, generation) operates on top of it.

Historical evolution
Pre-2022 · Information Retrieval era
Lexical search: the document is the unit
Traditional search engines (BM25, TF-IDF) indexed whole documents. Retrieval returned "Document 42 is relevant." Summarising or extracting specific facts required the user to read the document themselves.
2022–2023 · First-generation RAG
Fixed-size chunking becomes the default
As LLMs entered production, engineers needed to feed relevant text into the prompt. The simplest approach: split every document into N-token blocks, embed each block, store in a vector DB, retrieve top-k. Crude, but it worked well enough to ship.
2023 · LlamaIndex benchmarks
First empirical data on chunk size
LlamaIndex ran a systematic study on Uber's 10-K filing, benchmarking sizes from 128 to 2048 tokens. Finding: 1024 tokens produced peak faithfulness. Smaller lost context; larger introduced noise. This became the first evidence-based default.
2024 · Semantic chunking & smarter approaches
Meaning-aware splitting; Anthropic's Contextual Retrieval
Semantic chunking used embedding similarity to detect topic shifts instead of cutting at fixed intervals. Anthropic introduced Contextual Retrieval: prepend a 50–100 token LLM-generated summary to each chunk before embedding — reportedly cutting top-20 retrieval failures by up to 67% with reranking. Jina introduced Late Chunking: embed the full document first, then split.
Jan 2026 · arXiv:2601.14123
The context cliff & the overlap myth
Bennani & Moslonka's systematic analysis challenged two long-held assumptions: (1) overlap is always helpful — it isn't, in their setup; (2) bigger context is always better — response quality degrades past ~2,500 tokens. Both findings are from a single study and need replication, but they've already shifted practitioner defaults.
The semantic chunking trade-off

Why smarter isn't always better

Chonkie benchmarks: semantic chunking runs at ~0.33 MB/s versus ~4.82 MB/s for token-based — roughly 14× slower. For a 10 GB corpus, that's hours versus minutes.
— Chonkie library benchmarks, cited in Digital Applied, May 2026
The January 2026 arXiv analysis found sentence chunking matched semantic chunking on performance up to ~5,000 tokens at far lower cost.
— Bennani & Moslonka, arXiv:2601.14123
The principle behind this: Semantic chunking is solving the problem of "cuts that land in the middle of a topic." But if your topics tend to be short (say, under a couple of paragraphs), sentence boundaries already capture the same information. You only need semantic detection when topic shifts happen at unpredictable intervals — dense academic papers, mixed-purpose documents, dialogue transcripts. For most structured corporate content, sentence boundaries are a cheap proxy.

The Mechanism

Why does a context cliff exist at 2,500 tokens? And why does overlap fail to help?

What follows is a conceptual model explaining the likely mechanisms. The empirical findings come from the arXiv paper; the explanatory model is a generated illustration.
Mechanism 1 · The context cliff

Why more tokens ≠ better answers

When a RAG system retrieves top-k chunks and concatenates them into the context window, two competing forces are at play:

Signal vs noise trade-off:

Every chunk you add brings some probability of containing the answer (signal), but also introduces text that doesn't relate to the query (noise). As total context grows, the noise accumulates faster than the signal — because the marginal chunk is increasingly unlikely to be the one containing the answer.

At small sizes, more context genuinely helps — you're filling in missing information. Past a threshold (~2,500 tokens in Bennani & Moslonka's setup), the noise starts degrading the model's ability to locate the relevant portion. This is sometimes called the "lost-in-the-middle" effect: LLMs attend more strongly to tokens at the beginning and end of their context, and information in the middle gets underweighted.
The January 2026 arXiv study found BERTScore declined ~4–5% relatively at 10k tokens for sentence chunking compared to optimal context length. Performance improved from small to moderate contexts, then degraded past ~2,500 tokens.
— Bennani & Moslonka, arXiv:2601.14123
The specific 2,500-token number is from one experimental setup (SPLADE retrieval + Mistral-8B + Natural Questions). The shape of the effect — quality rising then falling — is well-supported; the exact threshold will vary by model and domain.
Mechanism 2 · Why overlap fails

The intuition behind overlap — and why it breaks down

Why overlap was supposed to work:
Imagine a sentence that spans a chunk boundary. Without overlap, the left half is in chunk A and the right half is in chunk B. If only chunk A is retrieved, the sentence is incomplete. Overlap copies the tail of chunk A into the start of chunk B, ensuring the sentence appears whole in at least one chunk.

Why it often doesn't:
1. Indexing cost — overlap inflates the total number of tokens stored and embedded. A 20% overlap on a 1000-chunk corpus creates ~1200 chunks. More chunks = more embeddings = more storage = slower retrieval.

2. Duplicate retrieval — two highly similar chunks (the overlapping region) can both be retrieved, consuming context window space with near-identical text and reducing effective diversity of retrieved information.

3. The boundary case is rare — for most queries, the key information sits in the interior of a chunk, not at the boundary. Overlap pays a universal cost to solve an edge case.
Bennani & Moslonka's analysis found chunk overlap "provided no measurable benefit in their tested setup while raising indexing cost." This was on Natural Questions with SPLADE retrieval.
— arXiv:2601.14123, January 2026
Mechanism 3 · Why chunking outweighs model choice

Garbage in, garbage out — the retrieval floor

The analogy: Think of chunking as the quality of the raw materials going into a factory. An embedding model is the factory machinery. No matter how sophisticated your machinery, you cannot manufacture a precision part from sand. If the key fact about a question is split across two chunks and neither is retrieved, the generator has no path to the correct answer — regardless of how capable it is.

Conversely, a perfect retrieval set (all relevant facts, clearly bounded) lets even a modest generator produce a good answer. This is why the 9% recall gap from poor chunking choices is so consequential: it represents a ceiling on what the rest of the pipeline can achieve.
"When a RAG system performs poorly, the issue is often not the retriever — it's the chunks."
— Weaviate Engineering Team, Chunking Strategies for RAG, Sept 2025
What fixes context loss at boundaries

The smarter alternatives: Contextual Retrieval & Late Chunking

Anthropic's Contextual Retrieval (Sept 2024): A small model (Claude 3 Haiku) generates a 50–100 token description of each chunk's place in the document and prepends it before embedding. Contextual Embeddings alone reduced top-20 retrieval failures by ~35% (5.7% → 3.7%); with BM25, ~49% (→ 2.9%); with reranking, ~67% (→ 1.9%). Preprocessing cost: ~$1.02 per million document tokens with prompt caching.
— Anthropic, September 2024
Jina AI's Late Chunking (2024): Embeds all tokens of the full document first, then applies the split after the transformer. This preserves long-distance dependencies — anaphoric references like "its" or "the city" that point back to earlier sections survive the cut. BEIR benchmark gains: SciFact 64.20% → 66.10% nDCG@10; NFCorpus 23.46% → 29.98%.
— Michael Günther & Han Xiao, Jina AI, 2024
The conceptual difference between the two approaches:
Contextual Retrieval solves the problem at embedding time — it gives each chunk richer content before it's embedded, so the embedding itself reflects context.
Late Chunking solves it at representation time — it delays the split until after the model has seen the full document, so the chunk's vector already contains surrounding context.
Both add cost (LLM preprocessing or long-context embedding model requirements). Both are worth that cost only when your RAGAS metrics confirm context-loss is your bottleneck.
