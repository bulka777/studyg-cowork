The Effects of Tariffs on the Components of Inflation

Attribution: "The Effects of Tariffs on the Components of Inflation," FRBSF Economic Letter 2026-07, by Naomi Halbersleben, Òscar Jordà, and Fernanda Nechio. Federal Reserve Bank of San Francisco, March 30, 2026. Full text and PDF at frbsf.org.

Tariffs are usually applied to imported goods—but in an interconnected economy, their effects can be felt in the prices of other goods and services. Estimates using data across multiple advanced economies show that inflation declines right after tariffs are imposed. This initial decline reflects decreased demand, visible in declining prices for energy such as oil, a primary commodity typically exempt from tariffs. After the initial tariff shock, inflation gradually picks up, driven first by goods and later by services, one of the more persistent categories of inflation.

Background

The United States announced a wide range of import tariffs on its trading partners in early 2025. In earlier work, the authors showed that immediately following an increase in tariff rates, the unemployment rate tends to increase and inflation tends to fall—the hallmark of a negative demand shock. In such a scenario, consumers spend less and the economy slows down, but inflation also declines. Over time, the economy recovers, but inflation rises above what it would have been if there had been no change in tariffs.

Method

The analysis uses 40+ years of data from 16 advanced economies (Australia, Belgium, Denmark, Finland, France, Germany, Italy, Japan, Netherlands, Norway, Portugal, Spain, Sweden, Switzerland, UK, US). The experiment models a 10% increase in tariffs — close to estimates of the effective US tariff rate in 2025 (avg. 16.8% by Nov 2025, up from <2% during 2000–2024).

Tariffs and the Components of Inflation

The study examines four CPI components: energy, goods, services, and core (ex food & energy).

Energy (the initial drop)

Energy prices decline considerably on impact before settling back down. Energy is not directly tariffed, but oil is an internationally traded commodity. A decline in energy prices reflects a shortfall of demand — a slowdown in economic activity both domestically and internationally, consistent with rising unemployment immediately after a tariff shock.

Goods (slow build, peaks at year 2)

Initially, there is not much evidence of a pickup in goods inflation. The negative effects of tariffs on demand offset the increase in the cost of imported goods. However, goods prices peak at year 2, increasing 1.2 percentage points on average for a 10% tariff increase, and remain elevated through year 3.

Services (even slower, lingers)

Although services are not directly exposed to tariffs, part of the tariff increase still passes through gradually. Services inflation peaks ~0.6 pp higher in year 3 rather than year 2, and remains ~0.5 pp elevated by year 4. Services are stickier — they tend to linger.

Implications for the U.S. Economy

U.S. inflation continued to edge down in 2025, despite initial concerns. Our analysis suggests tariffs were associated with a reduction in U.S. economic activity, perhaps due to the rearrangement of supply chains and a wait-and-see attitude among investors and consumers. By end of 2025, the economy showed signs of resilience.

Importantly, the inflationary effects of tariffs could show up with some delay, especially for services. Services correspond to ~60% of the U.S. CPI basket. Although the effects on services are about half the size of goods effects, they could have a relevant impact on overall inflation — and may linger.

These estimates are based on 1980–2022 international data. The tariffs enacted since 2025 are unprecedented in magnitude and scope. Typical changes during the sample were much smaller. Interpret all quantitative figures with considerable caution. The authors state this explicitly.

Conclusion

The textbook prediction that tariffs cause a temporary increase in inflation doesn't hold simply in practice. Instead: (1) initial demand contraction → inflation falls, then (2) supply-chain repricing and pass-through → inflation gradually rises, with goods first and services last. The slow burn of services inflation — the largest component of the CPI — is the lasting risk.

🧠 Contextualized Learning
What is Inflation, really?
Inflation is a sustained rise in the overall price level, not a one-off price change. It reduces the purchasing power of money and is measured by indexes like the CPI (Consumer Price Index) or PCE (Personal Consumption Expenditures). Three main drivers: (1) demand-pull — too much spending chasing too few goods; (2) cost-push — rising input costs (wages, materials); (3) money growth — more money in circulation bidding up prices. (Source: Economics map rubric; consistent with article)
A one-off price rise is not inflation. If oil spikes for a month then recovers, that's a relative price shift — it changes what things cost relative to each other, not the overall level. Inflation means the average price level keeps rising period after period. The CPI tracks a "basket" of typical consumer purchases: food, housing, energy, cars, medical care, services. When that basket costs 3% more than last year, CPI inflation is 3%.

The Two-Stage Tariff Mechanism
Stage 1 (impact, ~year 0–1): Tariffs inject uncertainty → demand contracts → energy prices drop → headline inflation falls. This is a negative demand shock. Stage 2 (gradual, years 1–4): Firms absorb then pass through higher input costs → goods inflation rises (peak yr 2, +1.2 pp per 10% tariff) → services inflation follows and lingers (peak yr 3, +0.6 pp). Why does demand fall first? Tariffs create uncertainty. Firms don't immediately know their new cost structure. Consumers see news about trade wars and hold back spending. Importers rush to stockpile pre-tariff inventory (front-loading), which temporarily reduces current demand. Only once inventories deplete and firms revise prices does the inflationary signal emerge — hence the lag. This is the supply-and-demand mechanism played out over time.
Connecting to Supply & Demand (understood)
You know supply & demand shifts equilibrium. Here the tariff acts like a supply shock to imported goods (it raises the cost curve for goods that rely on imports), but simultaneously also a negative demand shock via uncertainty. These two forces partly cancel in the short run: higher costs push prices up; reduced demand pulls them down. The demand effect dominates initially (hence falling inflation). Over time, demand recovers but costs remain elevated, so goods prices rise. The interplay of these two curves explains the two-stage dynamic.
Business Cycle connection
The article finds tariffs are consistent with a negative demand shock: unemployment rises, inflation falls initially — hallmarks of a contraction phase in the business cycle. Later, as the economy adapts, activity and inflation recover. This maps to the expansion→peak→contraction→trough→expansion cycle. (Source: FRBSF article + earlier Halbersleben et al. 2025 findings on unemployment)

Monetary Policy Link (understood concept)
The article notes a "complicated dance between the lags of monetary policy and the implications of trade policy for inflation." If services inflation stays elevated for 3–4 years after a tariff shock, the Fed must decide whether to tighten (risking a deeper recession) or wait (risking entrenched inflation). This is exactly the dual-mandate tension — price stability vs. maximum employment — that monetary policy is designed to navigate.
