DCF & Equity Valuation: Stock Price as Present Value of Future Cash Flows

Attribution: "Walk Me Through a DCF: A Simple Guide to Discounted Cash Flow Valuation" by Sanzhi Kobzhan, Financial Modeling Prep, updated November 19, 2025. Full article at financialmodelingprep.com.

A DCF (Discounted Cash Flow) estimates a company's fair value based on the money it might generate in the future. By discounting these future cash flows back to today, we can see if the stock price is trading above or below its fundamental worth. If the current market price is lower than the DCF-derived price, it could be undervalued.

Step 1: Forecast the Free Cash Flows (FCF)

Project Free Cash Flows for typically 5–10 years. FCF is the cash left over after all business costs — operating expenses, taxes, and capital expenditures — are covered. It's the cash truly available to investors.

  • Identify Revenue and Growth: estimate sales from past performance and future outlook
  • Deduct Operating Costs and Taxes: arrive at EBIT, then tax-effect it
  • Adjust for Depreciation, Amortization, and CapEx
  • Consider Working Capital Changes (receivables, payables, inventories)

Step 2: Determine the Discount Rate (WACC)

The discount rate, often the Weighted Average Cost of Capital (WACC), represents how much return investors expect for the risk of investing in this company. Components:

  • Cost of Equity: derived using CAPM (risk-free rate + beta × market risk premium)
  • Cost of Debt: the interest rate on loans/bonds, adjusted for taxes
  • Capital Structure Weights: debt vs. equity proportion

Step 3: Calculate the Terminal Value

Companies don't vanish after the forecast period. The Terminal Value (TV) captures all cash flows beyond year 5–10, typically using the perpetuity growth approach:

TV = (Final Year FCF × (1 + g)) / (WACC − g)

where g is the long-term growth rate (typically 2–4%). Terminal value often represents 60–80% of total DCF valuation — it dominates.

Step 4: Discount All Cash Flows to Present Value

Discount each year's FCF plus the Terminal Value back to today:

PV = FCF_n / (1 + WACC)^n

Sum of all discounted amounts = Enterprise Value (EV).

Step 5: Get to Equity Value Per Share

Convert Enterprise Value to what's available to shareholders:

Equity Value = EV − Net Debt
Fair Value Per Share = Equity Value ÷ Shares Outstanding

Common DCF Mistakes

  1. Unrealistic growth rates — small changes in g or WACC swing valuation enormously
  2. Ignoring terminal value sensitivity — it's 60–80% of the answer; test it
  3. Mixing FCFF and FCFE — FCFF = cash to all capital providers; FCFE = only to equity holders
  4. Forgetting the business story — DCF is parameterized by judgment about the future
  5. No sensitivity analysis — always test what happens if WACC +1% or g −0.5%
🧠 Contextualized Learning
The Core Insight: Stock = PV of Future Cash Flows
The fundamental claim of DCF valuation: a stock's intrinsic value equals the present value of all future cash flows it is expected to generate, discounted at a rate reflecting the opportunity cost and risk of holding it. If the market price is below this value, the stock is undervalued; above it, overvalued. (Source: FMP article; consistent with CFA Institute valuation framework)
You already know (from money-time-value) that a dollar today > a dollar tomorrow because today's dollar can be invested. Now apply that same logic in reverse: if a company will generate $100 in free cash next year, and you could instead earn 10% risk-free elsewhere, you'd pay at most $100/(1.10) = $90.91 for that $100 today. A share of stock is just this reasoning applied to every future year's expected cash flows, summed up. That sum is the stock's intrinsic value.
Intrinsic Value of a Stock
V = FCF₁/(1+r)¹ + FCF₂/(1+r)² + ... + FCF_n/(1+r)^n + TV/(1+r)^n
where r = discount rate (WACC), TV = terminal value, n = forecast horizon

The 5-Step Pipeline
1
Forecast FCF (years 1–N)
Revenue projections → subtract costs, taxes, capex → free cash available to investors
2
Choose Discount Rate (WACC)
Blends cost of equity (CAPM) + cost of debt, weighted by capital structure. Reflects required return given risk.
3

Captures all cash flows after year N. Dominates total value (60–80%). TV = FCF_N × (1+g) / (WACC - g)

P/E Multiples vs. DCF — Why Both Matter
P/E (Price-to-Earnings) is the most common multiple: stock price ÷ earnings per share. It's a shortcut: if similar companies trade at 20× earnings and this one earns $5/share, a rough valuation is $100/share. DCF is more rigorous but requires more assumptions. (Source: FMP article + FMP education resources)
P/E is a compressed DCF. When investors say "this stock is worth 20× earnings," they're implicitly assuming a certain growth rate, discount rate, and duration — they just haven't spelled them out. DCF makes those assumptions explicit and testable. The danger with P/E alone: the multiple can change (markets reprice risk premiums), but DCF grounds you in the actual cash the business generates. A stock might look "cheap" at 10× P/E but be expensive in DCF terms if earnings quality is poor or growth is declining.
Risk & Return Connection (understood concept)
The WACC is essentially your risk-return framework applied to a firm. A riskier firm (higher beta, uncertain cash flows) demands a higher WACC → each future dollar is discounted more aggressively → the stock is worth less today for the same projected cash flows. This is why high-growth tech companies with uncertain revenues trade at wide P/E ranges: a 1% change in WACC can move the DCF value by 20%+. The discount rate isn't a technicality — it is the risk-return tradeoff you already understand, applied to every dollar of future earnings.
DCF is extremely sensitive to terminal value assumptions. Since terminal value is typically 60–80% of total EV, a 0.5% change in the long-term growth rate g can swing valuation by 15–25%. Practitioners always run sensitivity tables: what if WACC is 9% vs. 11%? What if g is 2% vs. 4%? Never trust a single-point DCF without sensitivity analysis.
