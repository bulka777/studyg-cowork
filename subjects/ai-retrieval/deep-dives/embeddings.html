1. What is an embedding?

An embedding converts a piece of text into a fixed-length list of numbers — a vector. That's the mechanical definition. The useful definition is different: an embedding is a coordinate in a high-dimensional space where proximity encodes semantic similarity.

Simplified illustration
Imagine collapsing all possible meanings of English onto a map. Words with similar meanings appear in the same neighbourhood. "Cat" and "kitten" are a block apart. "Cat" and "photosynthesis" are on opposite coasts. An embedding is just your address on that map — expressed as numbers.
Sourced — IBM / Wikipedia
Word embeddings are numerical representations of words in a high-dimensional vector space that capture semantic relationships based on usage patterns in large corpora. They represent "word complexities mathematically, which helps computers process and understand language more like humans do." — IBM Think

2. The geometry of meaning

Because meaning is now a point in space, you can use geometry to ask questions that were previously impossible with keyword search.

2D projection of a ~300-dimensional space. Real distances are higher-dimensional and richer. Click a group to highlight.

Measuring closeness: cosine similarity

The most common measure isn't Euclidean distance (straight-line) — it's cosine similarity: the cosine of the angle between two vectors. Two vectors pointing in the same direction score 1.0 (identical); perpendicular vectors score 0; opposite directions score −1.

Why cosine, not distance?
A 10,000-word document might have the same meaning as a 100-word summary, but its embedding magnitudes would differ. Cosine ignores magnitude and asks only: are these vectors pointing the same direction? That makes it robust to length and scale differences.

Recent research (2025) shows the geometry of sentence embedding spaces is subtler than often assumed: "cosine similarity captures shallow commonalities or differences between sentence embeddings, which are not necessarily predictive of their performance on specific tasks." Cosine remains the standard, but semantic similarity is not perfectly captured by any single metric.

3. How embeddings are learned

No one hand-codes these vectors. They emerge from a prediction task on massive text. The key idea is the distributional hypothesis:

Sourced — Word2Vec / Wikipedia
"You shall know a word by the company it keeps." (J.R. Firth, 1957) — Words that appear in the same contexts tend to have similar meanings. This is the foundation of all embedding methods.

Word2Vec: Skip-gram

The classic training method. Given a target word, the model tries to predict the surrounding context words. For every (target, context) pair observed in the corpus, the model nudges those two vectors closer together. Over millions of examples, words that consistently share context end up near each other in space.

Skip-gram training — step through it

Simplified illustration
Think of it as arranging words on a magnetic board. Two words that keep appearing near each other in sentences slowly get pulled together. "Dog" and "leash" appear in the same sentences constantly → they drift close. "Dog" and "algorithm" rarely co-occur → they stay distant. Do this for a trillion tokens and the geometry becomes remarkably semantic.
Sourced — Word2Vec Wikipedia / Baeldung
Word2Vec trains a shallow two-layer neural network with a projection layer. The weights of the projection layer are the embeddings — the network is discarded after training; only the weight matrix is kept. Two architectures exist: Skip-gram (target → predict context, better for rare words) and CBOW (context → predict target, faster to train).

Modern sentence encoders

Word2Vec gives one vector per word. Modern models (BERT, sentence-transformers) process the full sequence and produce a context-sensitive vector: "bank" in "river bank" gets a different vector than "bank" in "savings bank." The mechanism is the transformer's attention — each word can attend to all others before its vector is computed.

4. What the dimensions actually mean

Each number in the vector corresponds to one "dimension" in the learned space. Here's the uncomfortable truth:

The dimensions have no human-readable labels.
Dimension 412 doesn't mean "animal-ness." It's a learned feature that the network found useful for the prediction task. Some dimensions correlate loosely with interpretable properties (gender, tense, formality), but most are entangled combinations of many things. The geometry works, even if we can't read the axes.

"Linguistic information is encoded in weighted combinations of different dimensions, which are not reflected in the geometry of the sentence embedding space." The embedding dimensions are not orthogonal, interpretable features — they're learned projections that collectively encode meaning.

5. From words to sentences

The RAG systems you're learning about don't embed individual words — they embed passages. Models like text-embedding-3-small (OpenAI) or all-MiniLM-L6-v2 (open source) encode an entire sentence or paragraph into a single vector.

Sourced — John Snow Labs / Sentence Transformers
Sentence transformer models produce embeddings (typically 384 to 4096 dimensions) suitable for similarity computation using cosine similarity. They are trained to make semantically similar sentences cluster together in vector space — enabling search by meaning rather than by term matching.

The workflow in a retrieval system:

  1. Offline: embed every chunk of your document corpus → store vectors in a vector store
  2. At query time: embed the user's question → find the chunks with the nearest vectors → return them as context

This is why embeddings are the engine of semantic search — and why they're the prerequisite for understanding vector stores and RAG.

6. Limitations (the honest part)

Where embeddings struggle
  • Exact-match terms: BM25 still beats embeddings on rare proper nouns, product codes, and technical IDs — "SKU-4471-B" hashes perfectly in an inverted index; an embedding may blur it.
  • Negation: "not happy" and "happy" may embed close together because both sentences share most context words.
  • Out-of-distribution: a model trained on Wikipedia will produce poor embeddings for highly specialised jargon it never saw.
  • Cost: computing embeddings at scale requires GPUs; inverted indexes are CPU-friendly and cheap.

This is why production systems combine both approaches — hybrid retrieval (a later concept in your map).
